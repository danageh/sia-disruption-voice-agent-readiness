# Current Workflow Map

## Project Scope

Direct SIA-booked customers affected by Typhoon Jangmi retimed flights, focused on refund and reaccommodation triage.

## Current Customer Journey

| Step | Customer Action | Likely Support Need |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Receives disruption notice | Sees that flight has been retimed | Understand what changed |
| 2. Checks new timing | Compares new departure/arrival against plans | Confirm exact flight, date, and destination |
| 3. Assesses impact | Checks hotel, onward flight, work, visa, family, or trip plans | Decide whether to accept the new timing |
| 4. Looks for options | Searches SIA site, flight status page, refund page, or assistance form | Ask for refund or alternative flight |
| 5. Contacts support | Calls, submits form, or uses digital support | Wants a clear next step |
| 6. Waits for resolution | Support verifies booking, route, ticket rules, disruption status, and request type | Needs confirmation and follow-up |

## Current Support Workflow

| Step | Support Team Action | Possible Blocker |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Identify customer | Confirm name, booking reference, flight number, and travel date | Customer gives incomplete or wrong details |
| 2. Confirm disruption | Check whether the flight is one of the retimed flights | Flight data may change |
| 3. Check booking channel | Confirm direct SIA booking versus travel agent or partner airline | Customer may not know who issued the ticket |
| 4. Understand request | Refund, reaccommodation, status check, missed connection, or escalation | Customer may mix multiple issues |
| 5. Check appropriate next step | Review available public guidance and internal workflow rules | Refund and rebooking rules may be conditional |
| 6. Route next step | Assistance form, alternative flight handling, human agent, or travel-agent referral | Wrong routing causes repeat contact |
| 7. Record context | Capture reason, customer preference, flight, urgency, and open questions | Poor notes make handoff weak |

## Where the Workflow Breaks

| Breakpoint | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Flight-number confusion | Multiple flights were affected across different Japan routes and dates. |
| Date confusion | Some flights depart on one date and arrive the next day. |
| Booking-channel confusion | Direct SIA bookings and travel-agent bookings follow different paths. |
| Refund ambiguity | Customers may assume they are entitled to a refund, but the next step depends on booking source and applicable rules. |
| Reaccommodation complexity | Alternative flights depend on availability, destination, timing, and customer preference. |
| Emotional escalation | Weather disruption affects hotels, connections, work, family plans, and trip purpose. |
| Stale information risk | Flight timings can change again during severe weather disruption. |
| Weak handoff | If the agent escalates without structured context, the human agent repeats discovery. |

## Voice-Agent Implication

The voice agent should not be positioned as a full disruption-resolution agent on day one.

It should start as a triage and routing agent that can:

- identify the customer’s affected flight
- confirm whether the booking is direct with SIA
- explain the customer’s likely next-step path
- collect refund or reaccommodation preference
- route direct-booking customers to the right next step
- escalate complex cases with a structured handoff summary

It should not independently approve refunds, compensation, policy exceptions, or complex reaccommodation.

## Workflow Verdict

The value is not just answering questions faster. The value is reducing support load while preserving accuracy, escalation quality, and customer trust during disruption.
